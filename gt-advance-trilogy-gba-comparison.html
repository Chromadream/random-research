🏁 GT Advance Championship Racing Game 1

📦 Overview

The game that started it all. GT Advance Championship Racing (known in Japan as Advance GTA) was a launch title for the Game Boy Advance in North America. It was developed by MTO and published by THQ after a reported bidding war for the Western publishing rights — the Japanese version had been received so positively that multiple US publishers competed for it.

🎮 Key Features

  • 48 licensed Japanese cars from 8 manufacturers (Honda, Toyota, Nissan, Mazda, Mitsubishi, Subaru, Suzuki, Daihatsu)
  • 32 tracks across 5 categories: circuit, highway, city, winding, and dirt
  • 9 upgradeable car categories: engine, suspension, body weight, muffler, CPU, limited-slip differential, air filter, seats, and chassis — some upgrades visually change the car (e.g., adding an aero kit to a Skyline GT-R gives it a taller wing)
  • 4 championship difficulty levels (Beginner through Expert), each with 8 tracks
  • Unlockable modes: Kart Racing (Extra 1) and Formula Racing (Extra 2)
  • Manual or automatic transmission (L/R to shift)
  • Powersliding essential for cornering

⚠️ The Infamous Password Save System

The single most notorious thing about GT Advance 1. The Japanese version (Advance GTA) had a battery-backed save — but THQ removed the battery RAM from the North American and European releases and replaced it with a 16-character password system using upper/lowercase letters, numbers, and symbols. Critics universally panned this cost-cutting move. GameSpot went so far as to recommend importing the Japanese version instead (since the menus were in English and there was no region lock).

🏔️ GT Advance 2: Rally Racing Game 2

📦 Overview

GT Advance 2: Rally Racing (known in Japan as Advance Rally) took a hard pivot from street racing to the world of rally racing. It's arguably the most different of the three games — not just an iteration but a full genre shift. Released about a year after the first game, it addressed the original's biggest complaint head-on.

🆕 What Changed from GT Advance 1

  • 🔴 The biggest fix: BATTERY SAVE. THQ restored the battery-backed save after the backlash from the first game. No more writing down 16-character passwords.
  • Completely different racing discipline: Rally racing replaces circuit/street racing. Cars race on tarmac, gravel, mud, and snow.
  • Fewer cars, more focused: 15 licensed rally cars (Subaru Impreza, Mitsubishi Lancer Evo, Toyota Celica, etc.) instead of 48 street cars.
  • More tracks: 42 tracks across 14 real-world rally stages (Sweden, Finland, France, Cyprus, Australia, etc.) — each event has 3 "legs."
  • Performance upgrades REMOVED: You can no longer buy/install aftermarket parts. Instead, you fine-tune existing parameters: suspension stiffness, steering speed, gear ratio, and brake balance.
  • Tire selection: Must choose tires before each leg — slicks for tarmac, all-terrain for mixed surfaces. A real rally strategy element.
  • Elevation & jumps: The scaling engine was enhanced for rally terrain — tracks now have bumps, dips, and elevation changes. You can catch air!
  • Co-Driver / Navigator mode: A totally unique mode where you don't drive — you call out turn directions to an AI driver using button/D-pad combos. Get it wrong and you crash.
  • License tests: 15 tests to familiarize you with rally driving mechanics.
  • Race structure: "Catch-up" format — opponents are staggered ahead, and you race to overtake them, unlike real rally (which is time-based).
  • Graphics improved: More peripheral objects (trees, shrubbery), varied weather conditions.

⚠️ What Critics Didn't Like

  • Removal of the upgrade system made the game feel more shallow than the first
  • Game was considered too short — could be beaten in a single day
  • Unlocking new cars felt less rewarding since a top-tier car (Subaru Impreza) was available from the start
  • Sound quality took a step back — less distinct engine sounds between cars, occasional audio cutouts

⚡ GT Advance 3: Pro Concept Racing Game 3

📦 Overview

GT Advance 3: Pro Concept Racing (known in Japan as Advance GT 2) is widely considered the true sequel to the original. It returns to paved-street racing but hybridizes the best of both predecessors: the city environments and upgrade systems of GT1 with the slippery, drift-friendly physics of GT2. It's the most content-rich game in the trilogy.

🆕 What Changed from GT Advance 1 & 2

  • Massive car roster: 97 cars — more than double GT1's count. Manufacturers include Honda, Mazda, Mitsubishi, Nissan, Subaru, Toyota, and more.
  • Upgrades return: 8 categories (engine, muffler, suspension, limited-slip differential, air filter, seats, CPU, weight reduction). Earned through 1st and 2nd place finishes.
  • Hybrid physics engine: Paved streets like GT1, but slipperier handling borrowed from GT2's rally DNA. Drifting and fishtailing are now integral to winning, not optional.
  • Lenient collisions: Invisible walls are gone. Hitting off-road grass/dirt only slows you slightly. Hitting other cars is less punishing. Much more forgiving than GT1.
  • Drift Combo mode: Brand-new mode where you chain power-slides to earn points and unlock cars. A direct result of the driftier physics.
  • License tests return & expand: 5 championship classes, each requiring 3 license test situations before you can race. More structured progression.
  • Longer, more complex tracks: U-turns, S-curves, combination hairpins. Nighttime and rain races. 40 tracks total.
  • Camera improvements: Camera pulled back slightly for a less claustrophobic view. Road surfaces have added texture.
  • Unlockable kart mode: Completing all five championship classes unlocks kart racing.
  • Battery save retained (lesson learned from GT1).

⚠️ Lingering Issues

  • Tracks still flat — no hills or elevation like GT2
  • Soundtrack still used the GBA's lesser audio channels (described as "NES-quality")
  • Repetitive track design after extended play
  • By 2003, competitors like Colin McRae Rally 2.0 had better graphics engines

🔬 Deep Dive: The Three Biggest Changes Across the Trilogy

1. 💾 The Save System Saga

This is the defining story of the trilogy. THQ's decision to rip out battery-backed saves from GT Advance 1 became the cautionary tale of early GBA publishing. Critics across the board — IGN, GameSpot, Eurogamer, Nintendo Power — all hammered the password system. GameSpot's review literally told readers to import the Japanese version instead. THQ listened: both GT Advance 2 and 3 restored battery saves. It's a rare case of a publisher fixing a universally-hated decision across sequels.

2. 🏎️ Racing Philosophy: Simulation → Arcade Drift

The trilogy drifted (pun intended) further from realism with each entry:

  • GT1: Gran Turismo-lite. Realistic handling, upgrade trees, tight cornering. Powersliding was a technique to master — but you could race clean.
  • GT2: Rally authenticity. Tire selection, surface variety, co-driver calls. Real-world rally stages. But the upgrade loop vanished.
  • GT3: Ridge Racer meets Gran Turismo. Slippery physics require drifting. The Drift Combo mode makes it explicit. Collisions are forgiving. It's the most arcade-like of the three.

3. 📈 Content Progression: Peak at GT3

GT Advance 3 is the most feature-complete game by a wide margin: 97 cars (vs. 48 and 15), 40+ tracks, upgrades, license tests, drift combo, kart mode, battery save, better camera, weather effects. It's the culmination of everything MTO learned across the first two games — though ironically, the aging engine and increased competition meant it scored lower than the first game.

🏷️ Naming Confusion: Japan vs. West

One oddity worth noting: the Japanese naming doesn't line up with the Western naming at all:

Western TitleJapanese TitleMeaning
GT Advance Championship RacingAdvance GTA"GTA" = Gran Turismo Advance?
GT Advance 2: Rally RacingAdvance RallyStraightforward — rally game
GT Advance 3: Pro Concept RacingAdvance GT 2Suggests it's the true sequel to Advance GTA

Japan considered Advance Rally a spin-off (not numbered) and Advance GT 2 the real sequel. THQ numbered all three sequentially in the West, which makes sense commercially but obscures the relationship.

📦 Bonus: The 4-in-1 Combo Cart

In 2007, a compilation cartridge was released: "4 Games on One Game Pak: GT Advance / GT Advance 2 / GT Advance 3 / MotoGP." This rare GBA cart bundled the entire trilogy plus THQ's MotoGP GBA game. A collector's curiosity and probably the most efficient way to own the whole series on original hardware.
