Attention Is All You Need

The paper that invented the Transformer — the architecture behind ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, and every modern AI language model. Explained for non-ML readers.

1 The Problem: Teaching Computers to Translate

Imagine you want a computer to translate "The cat sat on the mat"In ML, this input is called the source sequence — a list of words in the original language. from English to French: “Le chat s’est assis sur le tapis.”

This is called sequence transductionTurning one sequence (list) of things into another sequence. Translation is the classic example, but it also applies to summarization, question-answering, and more.. The computer needs to read the English sentence, understand it, and produce the French sentence — one word at a time. And the French sentence might be a different length than the English one.

Before 2017, the best tools for this job were Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)A type of neural network that processes words one-by-one in order, keeping a "memory" of what it has seen so far. Like reading a sentence left to right and maintaining a running summary.. They worked, but had a fatal flaw…

2 The Old Way: Reading Word by Word (RNNs)

An RNN reads a sentence one word at a time, updating a hidden stateA vector of numbers that represents the network's "memory" of everything it has read so far. It gets updated after each word. — like keeping a summary in your head as you read. But this is sequential: you can't process word #5 until you've finished word #4.

Problem 1: No parallelization. With an RNN, you must wait for each word in order. If your sentence is 100 words long, that's 100 sequential steps. A modern GPU can do thousands of calculations at once — but RNNs can't use that power.
Problem 2: Forgetting. When the RNN reaches word #50, it has trouble remembering what it saw back at word #3. The "memory" fades over distance, like a game of telephone.

People tried to fix these problems with convolutions (CNNs)Instead of reading one word at a time, a CNN looks at small groups of neighboring words at once (e.g., 3-word windows). This can be parallelized, but it can only "see" a limited range without many stacked layers. and various tricks, but nothing fully solved the core tension: reading in order is slow; reading out of order misses context.

3 The Big Idea: Attention

The paper’s title says it all: Attention Is All You Need. Their breakthrough was: what if every word directly looks at every other word, all at once?

Imagine you’re translating the sentence “The animal didn’t cross the street because it was too tired.” To translate “it” correctly into French (where animals and streets have different genders), you need to know what “it” refers to. That requires looking back at “the animal” — not the street.

AttentionA mechanism where each word computes a "relevance score" with every other word. Words that are related get higher scores, and their information is mixed together. This lets the model directly connect "it" to "animal" even if they are far apart. is a mechanism that allows the model to weigh the importance of every word relative to every other word. It computes a score: “how much should I care about word X when processing word Y?”

The key insight: Attention lets information flow directly between any two words, regardless of distance. The word “it” at position 10 can directly look at “animal” at position 2 — no fading, no sequential waiting.

The authors built an entire architectureThe overall design and structure of a neural network — what components it has and how they are connected. using only attention mechanisms, removing RNNs entirely. They called it the Transformer.

4.1 Step 1: Turn Words into Numbers

Computers don't understand words — they understand numbers. Each word is first converted into a vectorAn ordered list of numbers, like [0.3, -1.2, 0.7, ...]. In the Transformer, each word becomes a vector of 512 numbers that encodes its meaning. Words with similar meanings end up with similar vectors. (a list of 512 numbers) via an embeddingA learned mapping from words to vectors. The numbers are learned from data: "king" and "queen" end up with different but related vectors, capturing semantic relationships. layer.

4.2 Step 2: Add Position Information

Since attention looks at all words simultaneously, the model has no built-in sense of word order. Is "dog bit man" different from "man bit dog"? Absolutely. So the authors add positional encodingsA set of sine and cosine waves at different frequencies, added to each word's vector. Because the waves oscillate, each position gets a unique pattern. The model can learn to recognize positions from these patterns. Since sin(x+k) can be expressed as a function of sin(x), the model can even learn relative positions (word 5 relative to word 2, etc.). — a fixed pattern of sine and cosine waves that tells the model where each word sits in the sentence.

4.3 Step 3: Self-Attention (The Core)

This is the heart of the paper. For each word, the model computes three things:

  • Query (Q): “What am I looking for?”
  • Key (K): “What do I offer?”
  • Value (V): “What information do I carry?”

Think of it like a library search. The Query is your search topic. Each book has a Key (its title/subject) and a Value (its content). You match your Query against every Key to find relevance, then pull the Values of the most relevant books.

For the word "it" in "the animal didn't cross the street because it was tired":

  1. "it" generates a Query: "I'm looking for whatever I refer to."
  2. Every word generates a Key: "animal" keys say "I'm a living thing"; "street" keys say "I'm a place."
  3. The Query matches "animal"'s Key more strongly. That match score (between 0 and 1) is the attention weight.
  4. "it"'s final representation is a blend: mostly "animal"'s Value, a bit of everything else.

4.4 Multi-Head Attention

One attention pattern isn't enough. A single "it" might need to know its referent and the verb it's the subject of and the tense. So the Transformer runs 8 different attention mechanisms in parallel (8 "heads"). Each head can learn a different relationship type — syntactic, semantic, positional, etc. Their outputs are combined.

4.5 The Rest of the Recipe

After self-attention, each word goes through a Feed-Forward NetworkA simple two-step computation: the 512-number vector is expanded to 2048 numbers, a ReLU (a function that sets negative values to zero) is applied, then it's compressed back to 512. This provides additional processing capacity at each position independently. — the same simple transformation applied independently to every word position. Then the whole cycle repeats: self-attention → feed-forward → self-attention → feed-forward — 6 times.

Each layer has residual connectionsA "skip connection" that adds the original input of a layer to its output. This helps gradients flow through the network during training and allows the model to learn more effectively with many layers. The paper uses this + layer normalization after each sub-layer. (the input is added back to the output) and layer normalizationA technique that normalizes the values in a vector to have a stable mean and variance. This makes training more stable and faster., which help training stability.

4.6 The Decoder: Generating the Translation

The Decoder is similar to the Encoder, but with two key differences:

  1. Masked self-attention: When generating the third word of the French translation, the decoder can only look at words 1 and 2 — not words 4 or 5. It can't cheat by seeing the future. This is done by maskingIn the self-attention softmax, all positions after the current one are set to -infinity, so their attention weights become zero. The model is forced to generate left-to-right, one word at a time, which is the definition of an autoregressive model. future positions out.
  2. Encoder-Decoder attention: Each decoder position also attends to the entire Encoder output. This is how the translation knows what English words it's translating. The Queries come from the Decoder, but the Keys and Values come from the Encoder.

At the final layer, a softmaxA function that converts a vector of arbitrary numbers into probabilities (numbers between 0 and 1 that sum to 1). The model then picks the most likely word. function converts the decoder's output into probabilities over the vocabulary, and the highest-probability word is chosen.

6 Why Self-Attention Wins

The paper includes a head-to-head comparison of self-attention vs. RNNs vs. CNNs across three metrics. Here it is, simplified:

Metric Self-Attention RNN CNN
Can it parallelize? Yes — all words at once No — one word at a time Yes — all windows at once
Steps to connect any two words 1 step (direct) N steps (faded) log(N) steps (dilated)
Computational cost for N words N² (each word to each) N × 512² N × k × 512²

The cost column reveals a sweet spot: when the sentence is shorter than 512 words (almost always true for translation), self-attention is actually cheaper than RNNs. And even for longer sequences, the direct-connection benefit is enormous.

Bottom line: Self-attention is faster (parallel), more direct (1 step), and comparably cheap (for typical sentence lengths). This is the triple win that made the Transformer revolutionary.

9 Why This Paper Changed Everything

Before 2017, almost all AI language models used RNNs. After this paper, everything shifted to Transformers. Here's why:

  • Parallel training on massive datasets became feasible, leading to models that read the entire internet.
  • Long-range dependencies were no longer a problem, enabling coherent paragraphs and documents.
  • Transfer learning (pre-train on general data, fine-tune on specific tasks) became the standard approach.

Direct descendants of this paper include BERT (Google Search), GPT (ChatGPT, GPT-4), T5, LLaMA, and every model in the modern AI landscape. The "Attention" mechanism it pioneered is the foundation of the current AI revolution.

The punchline: The authors replaced a complex, sequential architecture with a simpler, parallel one that just looks at relationships between words. That simple idea — "Attention Is All You Need" — became the foundation of modern AI.

• Quick Reference Glossary

AttentionA mechanism that computes relevance scores between all pairs of words, allowing direct information flow regardless of distance.
Self-AttentionAttention where the source and target are the same sequence — each word looks at every other word in the same sentence.
Multi-HeadRunning 8 parallel attention mechanisms, each learning different types of word relationships.
Query / Key / ValueThe three vectors derived from each word. Query asks "what am I looking for?", Key says "what am I?", Value carries "my information."
Positional EncodingSine/cosine waves added to word vectors to give the model information about word order.
EmbeddingA learned mapping from words to 512-number vectors that capture meaning.
Encoder-DecoderEncoder reads the input; Decoder generates the output. The Decoder attends to the Encoder's output.
AutoregressiveThe model generates one word at a time, using previously generated words as input for the next.
Residual ConnectionA shortcut that adds a layer's input to its output, helping gradients flow and enabling deeper networks.
Layer NormalizationNormalizes values in a vector to stable mean/variance, making training faster and more stable.
MaskingPreventing the decoder from attending to future positions by setting attention weights to zero.
SoftmaxConverts numbers into probabilities that sum to 1 — used to pick the most likely next word.
