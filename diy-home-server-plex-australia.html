Idle power: 6–10W | Annual cost: ~$22–$38 AUD

Handles 3–5 simultaneous 4K→1080p transcodes via Quick Sync. Perfect for households with 2–4 users. Tiny, silent, sips power. Add external USB HDDs or a separate NAS for bulk storage.

Upfront cost: ~$900–$1,300 AUD (base system, no storage)

Idle power: 20–35W | Annual cost: ~$70–$120 AUD

Mini-ITX or mATX build with room for 6+ HDDs. Can handle 10–15 simultaneous 4K transcodes. Runs Plex, NAS, Docker containers, VMs — everything in one box.

Refurbished Dell PowerEdge or HP ProLiant rack servers (e.g., R720, DL380) can be found for $300–$600 AUD. Decision #3: Transcoding needs?

Direct Play (no transcoding) uses almost zero CPU. If all your clients support your media format (modern TVs, Apple TV 4K, NVIDIA Shield), you barely need any CPU.

Hardware Transcoding via Intel Quick Sync (integrated GPU) is the game-changer. A 6W N100 can transcode 4K HDR→1080p SDR with hardware tone mapping — something that would require a 17,000 PassMark CPU to do in software. Note: Plex hardware transcoding requires a Plex Pass subscription.

6. CPU Deep Dive: Intel Quick Sync & Transcoding

Why Intel (Not AMD) for Plex Transcoding

Plex officially supports Intel Quick Sync Video and NVIDIA NVENC for hardware transcoding. AMD's integrated graphics (VCE/VCN) are not officially supported by Plex and have limited/experimental support in Jellyfin. For a Plex-focused build, Intel is the clear choice.

Recommended HDDs for NAS Use

  • Seagate IronWolf / IronWolf Pro — Designed for 24/7 NAS use, 7200 RPM. ~$29–$31/TB in Australia (e.g., 12TB IronWolf Pro ~$368 from MSY).
  • Western Digital Red Plus — CMR (not SMR) versions only. Avoid standard WD Red (SMR) for RAID.
  • Seagate Exos — Enterprise-grade, often good value when on sale. Slightly louder.
  • "Shucking" — Buying external USB drives and removing the internal HDD can save 30–50%. Common in Australia with WD Elements/Easystore. Caveat: warranty voided, may need 3.3V pin mod.

11. What You Might Have Missed

🔌 Power Supply Efficiency at Low Loads

Most ATX PSUs are designed for gaming PCs and operate poorly at the very low loads a home server typically draws (10–50W). A 750W PSU at 3% load may only be 50–60% efficient — meaning your system draws 30W at the wall but only 15–18W reaches the components. Solutions:

  • Use a PicoPSU (DC-DC) with an external power brick — ~90% efficient at low loads
  • Choose an 80+ Platinum or Titanium rated PSU (tested down to 10% load)
  • The be quiet! Pure Power 12 M series specifies efficiency down to 2% load
  • Size your PSU appropriately — 450W is plenty for a server without a GPU

🌡️ HDD Cooling Matters

6 drives packed tightly in a Node 304 can hit 45–50°C under sustained load — shortening drive lifespan. Ensure good airflow: intake fans in front, exhaust in rear. Keep drives below 40°C for longevity. The Node 304's 3 included fans + a rear exhaust fan does this well.

📡 Remote Access & Security

Don't port-forward Plex directly. Use Tailscale (free for personal use) or WireGuard VPN for secure remote access. If you must expose Plex publicly, use a reverse proxy (Nginx Proxy Manager + Let's Encrypt) and enable 2FA on your Plex account.

🔋 UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply)

For a server running 24/7, a UPS protects against power surges and gives clean shutdown time. A basic 650VA UPS costs ~$100–$150 AUD. Connect via USB and configure automatic shutdown in Unraid/TrueNAS when battery is low.

🔄 Backup Strategy

RAID/parity is not backup. Follow the 3-2-1 rule: 3 copies, 2 different media, 1 offsite. For media that can be re-downloaded, this may be overkill — but for personal documents, photos, and configs, use cloud backup (Backblaze B2, ~$6/TB/month) or an external drive rotated offsite.
